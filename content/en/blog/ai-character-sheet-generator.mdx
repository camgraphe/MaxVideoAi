An AI character sheet generator is most useful when it does more than create a nice looking board.

It should create a **reusable reference asset** that helps later generations stay closer to the same face, outfit, and proportions.

That is the job of **[Consistent Character AI](/tools/character-builder)** inside MaxVideoAI.

## What makes a good AI character sheet

A useful sheet gives you a stable visual record of the character before you move into:

- new prompts
- scene changes
- alternate outfits
- storyboards
- image-to-video

At minimum, a strong sheet should help you preserve:

- face and hairstyle
- wardrobe silhouette
- proportions
- visible accessories
- distinctive details

If the sheet is too loose, you still end up solving the same identity problem over and over again.

## Why a character sheet matters before video

Image-to-video models do better when the first frame is already clear.

That first frame should not be the first time the model sees the character identity. It should be the moment when the identity is already settled.

This is why a reusable character sheet is useful even if your end goal is not a static image. It is a pre-production step for the rest of the workflow.

You can build the sheet in **[Consistent Character AI](/tools/character-builder)**, refine the selected output in **[Image](/app/image)**, and then move into **[Video](/app)** with a better first frame.

## Types of character sheet outputs

Different teams need different outputs. The most practical ones are:

### 3-view character sheet

Useful when you need a clear front-facing anchor and a stronger sense of how the character reads as a whole.

### Front / side / back turnaround

Useful for animation previs, comics, and any workflow where silhouette and body proportions matter.

### Portrait reference

Useful when face consistency is the priority and you plan to build scene variations later.

### Same character across multiple scenes

Useful when you are preparing campaign variants, recurring social content, or a short sequence.

## Who should use one

Character sheets are helpful for:

- creators building recurring characters
- marketers using recurring talent
- agencies producing multiple ad variants
- AI filmmakers planning scene continuity
- advanced hobbyists who want cleaner story workflows

The more often a character returns, the more valuable the sheet becomes.

## How to get better results

### Start with one strong source

Use the best single image you have rather than mixing several weak ones.

### Preserve the details that matter most

List the traits that cannot drift. Hair, wardrobe shape, accessories, and obvious facial cues usually matter more than abstract style language.

### Separate identity from scene styling

Solve the character first. Solve the environment later.

### Pick the right video model after the character is stable

Once the reference is ready, compare where to take it next in the **[Model Hub](/models)**. If you are building motion around a clear first frame, start by comparing **[Veo 3.1](/models/veo-3-1)**, **[Kling 3 Pro](/models/kling-3-pro)**, or **[Sora 2 Pro](/models/sora-2-pro)**.

## Character sheet vs prompt-only workflow

A prompt-only workflow can work for one-off images.

It becomes weaker when you need:

- multiple scenes
- repeatable ad creative
- a continuing story
- a stable first frame for motion

That is where a reusable sheet wins. It reduces the amount of identity you have to restate with every prompt.

## When to use a full sheet and when to use a portrait

Use a full sheet when:

- body shape matters
- wardrobe consistency matters
- you need turnarounds
- you are planning animation or multiple scene types

Use a portrait reference when:

- face identity is the main issue
- the shot will stay tighter
- you want a fast anchor before scene iteration

Many teams need both: a broad sheet for structure and a portrait for tight framing.
