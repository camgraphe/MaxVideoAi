## What this page is for
Fast draft work is about learning quickly. You are not trying to win final fidelity on the first run. You are testing the prompt, camera, composition, timing, and whether the idea deserves a higher-quality generation. The best draft model is cheap enough to run repeatedly and good enough to reveal the creative direction.

## Best picks
1. **[LTX 2.3 Fast](/models/ltx-2-3-fast)** - best for quick generation loops with 1080p to 4K options.
2. **[Seedance 2.0 Fast](/models/seedance-2-0-fast)** - best for fast Seedance-style reference tests and shot planning.
3. **[Veo 3.1 Fast](/models/veo-3-1-fast)** - best for quick Veo-style ad and image-to-video exploration.
4. **[Kling 3 Standard](/models/kling-3-standard)** - best for lower-cost testing of Kling 3 multi-shot and Elements workflows.

## Why these models rank here
LTX 2.3 Fast is the default when you need many fast passes. It keeps the LTX generation surface while being better suited to low-stakes iteration.

Seedance 2.0 Fast is the best draft option when you expect to final in Seedance 2.0. Use it to test timing, references, and shot logic before moving winners to the standard model.

Veo 3.1 Fast is useful for ad concepts and quick image-to-video checks. It lets you explore the Veo direction before committing to a more polished generation.

Kling 3 Standard is the right draft model when the final route is likely Kling 3 Pro. It gives you a lower-cost way to test whether the multi-shot structure and Elements anchors are working.

## Draft workflow
- Test one creative variable per run.
- Keep duration short unless timing is the thing being tested.
- Do not overpay for final resolution before the idea works.
- Promote only the best draft to Pro, Standard, or 4K final.
- Save the prompt structure that worked.

## FAQ
### What is the best AI video generator for fast drafts?
LTX 2.3 Fast is the best general draft engine, Seedance 2.0 Fast is best for Seedance final workflows, and Kling 3 Standard is best for testing Kling multi-shot structure.

### Should I draft in the same model I use for final output?
Often yes. Drafting in the same family reduces surprises when you move to the final model.

### When should I stop drafting?
Stop when the composition, timing, and subject behavior are working. Then move to the model that best fits final fidelity or resolution.
